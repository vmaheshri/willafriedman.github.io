Econ 7330: Introduction to Quantitative Methods

Instructor: Vikram Maheshri, vmaheshri@uh.edu, McElhinney 221A

TA: Weonhyeok Chung, weonhyeok.chung@gmail.com

Textbook: Statistical Inference, 2nd. Ed., Cassella and Berger

Course Overview

This course will provide some of the mathematical background that you will need to be a successful economist. The topics that we cover in this class will reappear frequently in your graduate coursework, the scholarly papers that you read, and the scholarly papers that you will someday write. The math in the course will be presented at a relatively high level of abstraction – probably much more than you have been exposed to in the past. This may make things difficult, but it is essential since the ultimate goal is to improve your mathematical fluency.

The course will be divided into five three week units as follows:

  1. Math background - techniques of mathematical proof, basic analysis and point-set topology

  2. A formal introduction to probability

  3. Random variables and their properties

  4. Random sampling and estimators

  5. Hypothesis testing

Grading

At the end of each unit, there will be an in class quiz. Together, quizzes 1-4 will be worth 60% of your grade, but I will drop your lowest score, i.e., Each be worth 20% of your grade. Quiz 5 will be worth 25% of your grade and cannot be dropped. Because the material in this course builds upon itself, all quizzes will be cumulative, but the emphasis will very clearly be placed on the newest material.

In addition to the quizzes, there will be 5 problem sets, each of which is worth 3% of your grade. I encourage you to work on problem sets in groups, but please write up your own solutions independently. Although the problem sets are not worth much, it is NOT in your interest to skip them, as quiz questions may bear a striking resemblance to questions on the problem sets. Please submit them electronically via email to Weonhyeok. After submitting problem sets, he will go over them in Friday section with you.

I don’t regularly schedule office hours because I’m pretty widely available. You should feel free to send me an email to schedule a time to come chat for any reason. Weonhyeok is also an invaluable resource, and you should get in touch with him if you want some extra help with something. We are both friendly, approachable and flexible, but it is your responsibility to come talk to us.

Proposed Schedule

I will adjust the pace of the course as needed. In general, Friday sections will only be held immediately following problem set submissions, but additional sections may be held if we need to make up a class. The section time is scheduled for Friday 9-10:30,

• Week 1 (8⁄20, 8⁄22) - introduction, basic logic, sets, functions, continuity

• Week 2 (8⁄27, 8⁄29) - sequences, convergence, contractions (PS1 due)

• Week 3 (9⁄3, 9⁄5) - Taylor’s theorem, Leibnitz’ rule, etc. QUIZ 1

• Week 4 (9⁄10, 9⁄12) - formal definitions and interpretations of probability, $\sigma$-algebras.

• Week 5 (9⁄20) - NO CLASS ON TUES/THURS. Makeup class on Friday during section. conditional probability, Bayes rule

• Week 6 (9⁄24, 9⁄26) - combinatorics, QUIZ 2 (PS2 due)

• Week 7 (10⁄1, 10⁄3) - introduction to random variables and distributions, moments of random variables,

• Week 8 (10⁄8, 10⁄10) - moment generating functions, properties of common distributions (PS3 due)

• Week 9 (10⁄15, 10⁄17) - common distributions, QUIZ 3

• Week 10 (10⁄22, 10⁄24) - multivariate distributions, random samples

• Week 11 (10⁄29, 10⁄31) - asymptotic properties of random samples, central limit theorem (PS4 due)

• Week 12 (11⁄5, 11⁄7) - moment estimators, maximum likelihood estimators, QUIZ 4

• Week 13 (11⁄12, 11⁄14) - bias in estimators, hypothesis testing

• Week 14 (11⁄19, 11⁄21) - hypothesis testing (PS5 due)

• Week 15 (11⁄26) - QUIZ 5
